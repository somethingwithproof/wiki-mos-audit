The '''Cascade Range''' (also called the '''Cascades''') is a major mountain range of western [[North America]], extending from northern [[California]] through [[Oregon]] and [[Washington (state)|Washington]] to southern [[British Columbia]].

The range includes both non-volcanic mountains and the [[High Cascades]], a younger volcanic chain that includes several of the most prominent peaks.

== Geology ==

The Cascades form part of the [[Pacific Ring of Fire]], the ring of volcanoes and associated mountains around the Pacific Ocean.<ref>{{cite book |last=Harris |first=Stephen L. |title=Fire Mountains of the West |publisher=Mountain Press |year=1988 |isbn=0-87842-220-X}}</ref>

The range was formed primarily through [[subduction]] of the [[Juan de Fuca Plate]] beneath the [[North American Plate]].<ref>{{cite journal |last=Tilling |first=Robert I. |title=Volcanic hazards |journal=USGS Circular |volume=1073 |year=1993 |pages=1–60}}</ref>

== Notable peaks ==

Several peaks in the Cascades exceed 3,000 meters in elevation. [[Mount Rainier]] in Washington is the highest at 4,392 meters. [[Mount Hood]] in Oregon reaches 3,429 meters.
